Product Detection

An iOS Application Utilizing Computer Vision
to detect distinct products

  • Unpublished Paper Available Upon Request

Abstract

Computer vision is becoming an increasingly critical area of research and its applications to real-world problems is growing at an incredible pace. We describe the design, development and evaluation of our computer vision iPad app for Sales Representatives in grocery store environments. Our system aims to assist Sales Reps to be more productive, reduce errors and provide increased efficiencies. We report on the creation of the iPad app, the design pattern for good detectors and the results of professional Sales Reps evaluating our system. Our system was tested in a variety of conditions in grocery store environments and has an accuracy of 96%, a SUS usability score of 85, and supports up to 40 detectors running concurrently to perform product identification in less than 5.9 seconds. We also created a design pattern to enable other researchers to create their own detectors for these types of products in complex environments (e.g., products with very similar packaging located on shelves).



The Problem

The way in which consumer packaged goods companies are keeping track of product availability and positioning at retail locations is not efficient. Currently, the most common approach involves having company representatives manually checking shelf layouts, stocks, position and orientation of products. Sales Reps assess stores to ensure they meet their client's standards and are compliant with contracts and protocols. At this time, virtually all of these processes are done manually with paper and pen using planograms (a diagram or model that indicates the placement of retail products on shelves to maximize sales). Of the relatively few apps available to support Sales Reps, they are limited in functionality and typically only address a small number of business-oriented questions using tablets.

Our Goals

The motivation behind this work is to enhance the use of mobile technologies in combination with computer vision to increase efficiencies of Sales Reps in the field. To this end, we set out to design and implement a system to accomplish this goal. The following list is the set of desirable characteristics we specified at the onset of the work:

  1. an advanced tool for Sales Reps to be more productive, reduce human errors, and increase their efficiencies;
  2. the detectors need to be accurate at recognizing objects of interest while discerning objects that are very similar (e.g., must distinguish between Minute Maid Original and Minute Maid Calcium);
  3. given an image of a grocery shelf containing potentially 50 different products, a successful detector must recognize at least one of several objects of interest (e.g., one Minute Maid Original from several on the shelf) and not identify any other products (no False Positives);
  4. the detectors need to be robust and operate successfully in a variety of conditions in grocery stores (e.g., variances in the lighting, orientation of products, obstructions, etc.);
  5. the implementation needs to be computationally efficient and run in real-time on the Sales Repâ€™s iPad without any network connectivity (the memory limitation is 1.5GB, and the system must be able to recognize up to 40 unique products within 20s)1;
  6. create a design pattern for the creation of training sets for other researchers to create their own detectors for similar types of products and environments (e.g. grocery shelves consisting of very similar products). The objective here is to create a repeatable design process that addresses similar problems.

Image Recognition - Tools and Technologies

One of our goals is to create accurate detectors that recognize objects of interest while discerning objects that are very similar. For example, the detector needs to distinguish between a Minute Maid Original product from a Minute Maid Calcium product. These two images in colour (as humans would see and differentiate between the two) and greyscale (as some computer vision systems see these images). The degree of similarities between these two images is quite subtle. It is difficult for the human eye to differentiate between these two amongst 50 similar projects on shelves. It is more difficult to design an algorithm to detect these differences.

After conducting a survey of candidate computer vision algorithms based on the following criteria: (1) accuracy, (2) computational speed, (3) memory efficiency, (4) ease of use (i.e., implementation with supporting documentation), and (5) portability (for mobile device use). We evaluated the following computer vision algorithms using this criteria: Cascade Object Recognition Algorithm, Mean Shift Segmentation, and Support Vector Machines. During the review and evaluation process, it became clear that the Cascade Object Recognition Algorithm was the most feasible since it provided the greatest potential to satisfy the requirements of the proposed research.

MATLAB was used to implement the Cascade Object Recognition Algorithm which we created a system for building object detectors relative to our products of interest. After testing during developement and once a level of 80% accuracy without false positives using real-life test images was achieved in MATLAB, we implemented the object recognition portion of our system as a native iPad app. The open source computer vision library (OpenCV) was used for the object recognition on the iPad. OpenCV is also used in MATLAB's Computer Vision System Toolbox, allowing our detectors to be ported from MATLAB to iOS.
